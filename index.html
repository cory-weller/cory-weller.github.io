Education

Graduate Work

Jan 2015 - Present

Bergland Lab @ UVA

My primary project began with developing a computationally efficient pipeline for reconstructing whole genomes from ultra-low coverage sequencing data in large, ephemeral, randomly outbred multi-parent populations that we call Hybrid Swarms. After achieving over 99.9% genotypic accuracy with less than 0.05X sequencing coverage (tested in simulated Drosophila Hybrid Swarms). I then simulated thousands of mapping populations to run through GWAS pipeline in order to compare mapping accuracy & precision of my new method to modern alternatives (such as the DGRP or DSPR).

Graduate Work

Jan 2014 - Dec 2015

Wu Lab @ UVA

As a comparative phylogenomics project in the Wu Lab, I investigated the relationship between evolutionary rates and bacterial spore-formation. Because most nucleotide substitutions are a result of errors during cell division, spore-forming bacteria (with their effectively longer durations between cell divisions), are expected to evolve more slowly. I developed a method of identifying putative spore-forming bacteria based on genome content, generated a large species tree, and performed independent contrasts of evolutionary rates dependent on spore-forming ability. The results clearly demonstrate a lower evolutionary rate for bacteria prediced to be spore-forming. DOI.

Undergraduate Work

May 2006 - Dec 2010

Gustavus Adolphus College

Undergraduate research included a survey of macroinvertebrate diversity and abundance in local ponds in southern Minnesota, depending on the presence of emergent aquatic vegetation, such as cattails. A second project involved modeling of various fish resource management strategies (i.e. catch-and-release, slot limit, or free-fishing), the results of which were presented at the college's Celebration of Creative Inquiry.
